## Introduction: Welcome to the Movement Challenge!

> Ready to integrate more movement into your daily life?

### Why Focus on Movement?

More and more studies show that movement is essential for the functioning of our body and brain. Our ancestors walked an average of 20 kilometers a day; this need for movement has largely disappeared, with all its consequences.

### What to Expect

For 15 days, you will be challenged to start each morning with a short physical exercise and to take at least your target number of steps daily. The first step is to set your personal goal!

## Day 1: Benefits of Movement

> **Saying:** "To keep your balance, you must keep moving."

**Summary:** This day emphasizes the clear benefits of sufficient movement on both physical and mental health, and the need to push your body to find a new, fitter balance.

### Sections
- **More Energy:** Temporary fatigue from exercise does not lead to the long-term fatigue of sitting still; fitness helps you endure everything better and feel more energetic.
- **Better Concentration:** The brain works best with sufficient movement; exercise helps find the calm needed for concentration when it's lacking.
- **Better Decision-Making:** Fitness increases your capacity and allows for more decisions, making you less limited.
- **Better Memory:** Body gymnastics is brain gymnastics; movement stimulates blood flow to your body and brain.
- **Better Stress Management:** Mental resistance increases, allowing you to persist longer with frustrating tasks, have more patience, and get into conflicts less often.
- **Physical Health:** You can take a beating better; the fitter you are, the better you can handle an accident.
- **Better Resistance:** People who move enough get sick less often and are less prone to injuries and back problems.
- **Better Mood:** Movement releases substances that give a positive boost, contribute to happiness, and suppress anxiety, stress, and sadness.
- **Better Quality of Life:** You feel better, can enjoy fun things longer, and get sick less often.

**Read More:** Despite the benefits, moving takes effort and the body resists the extra work. Perseverance is needed until the body finds a new balance. Creativity is needed to make room in a busy schedule, but movement can be well combined with relaxation and friends.

## Day 2: Incorporating More Movement

> **Saying:** 'Put on your walking shoes.'

**Summary:** A day full of practical tips to integrate more movement into daily life through planning, goal setting, utilizing travel time and breaks, and making movement more enjoyable and social.

### Sections
- **Routine:** Make a movement plan and put it in your calendar to increase the chance of actually moving.
- **Set a Goal:** Determine what you want to achieve with realistic, small, and achievable monthly goals. Schedule them, re-evaluate monthly, and reward yourself for each goal met.
- **Start the Day with an Exercise:** A short morning exercise loosens muscles and joints, making you more active throughout the day.
- **Utilize Your Break:** Use your break for a walk and daylight; you'll come back fitter for the rest of your workday.
- **Use Your Commute for Movement:** Walk and cycle whenever possible, or get off one stop earlier. Using travel time for this helps you relax more.
- **Move Together:** Moving together helps lower the barrier to procrastinate, and you spend time with your support group.
- **Sports Club:** Make movement your hobby; working together in a sport makes it easier to push your limits.
- **Nutrition and Movement:** Healthy, balanced nutrition helps you move better. Regular meals at fixed times provide stable energy. Eat plenty of fruits and vegetables for recovery.
- **Music:** Music can get you moving and help you keep it up longer. A dance hour is an option.
- **Find Guidance:** A coach who helps set and achieve goals is a better investment than spending money on relaxation.
- **Make It Fun:** See tedious tasks, like household chores, as movement. Your perspective affects your body, and people who see it this way achieve more physical benefits.

**Read More:** Look for added value (family walk, dance hour, discussing work problems while walking). Every step helps build a healthier life; even vacuuming, weeding, or doing planks/push-ups between appointments.

## Day 3: Disadvantages of Insufficient Movement

> **Saying:** 'Moving backwards.'

**Summary:** Focuses on the rapid and persistent negative consequences of inactivity on the body, from muscle and bone loss to mental and digestive issues, and the vicious cycle it creates.

### Sections
- **Muscles:** Rapid loss of muscle mass with inactivity. It takes much longer to regain strength and fitness than it took to lose them.
- **Bones:** Become weaker, increasing the risk of fractures in a fall. Rebuilding takes longer than losing. Strong bones are largely in your own hands.
- **Blood Vessels:** Lose their elastic adaptation with little movement, causing cells to quickly reach the limit of insufficient fuel.
- **Digestion:** Dependent on muscle activity. Inactive people often have trouble with bowel movements, and all organs function less effectively.
- **Brain:** With inactivity, the brain produces fewer substances that give a feeling of happiness, leading to sluggishness, gloominess, and less motivation; inactivity sustains itself.
- **Skin:** Poorer blood circulation and fluid retention, which can cause swelling, damage, and discoloration.

**Read More:** Inactivity sustains itself; it takes effort to break the cycle. As the body becomes weaker, it becomes increasingly difficult to break the situation.

## Day 4: Movement and Physical Health

> **Saying:** 'Rest rusts.'

**Summary:** Emphasizes that insufficient movement increases the risk of many lifestyle diseases and premature death, while sufficient movement acts as a powerful medicine to keep the body healthy.

### Sections
- **Heart Diseases:** Heart and blood vessels stay fit, and cholesterol is lowered. Inactivity increases the risk of a blocked blood vessel, which can lead to a heart attack or stroke.
- **Blood Pressure:** Movement lowers blood pressure; a good step to lower blood pressure without medication.
- **Overweight:** Very important for a healthy weight; being overweight carries many health risks.
- **Sickness:** Less often sick, with improved resistance, less inflammation, and a decrease in the risk of chronic disease and serious infections.
- **Diabetes:** The risk of diabetes is increased with insufficient movement; sufficient movement is extra important for chronic conditions.
- **Cancer:** The risk of some forms of cancer increases with insufficient movement.
- **Premature Death:** The risk of death is increased with insufficient movement.
- **Brain Diseases:** Insufficient movement increases the risk of dementia, Parkinson's, and stroke.
- **Back Complaints:** Fewer complaints of muscles and joints, and less frequent low back and neck pain.

**Read More:** Many lifestyle diseases can be prevented by sufficient movement. Health is our greatest asset and must be maintained.

## Day 5: Movement and Resistance

> **Saying:** 'With every step.'

**Summary:** Quantifies the positive influence of movement on resistance. Sufficient movement makes the body and immune system function optimally and significantly reduces the chance of getting sick.

### Sections
- **A Lot of Movement:** The chance of catching a cold is significantly smaller. Recovery is faster, and symptoms are less severe. Recovery after an accident or surgery is quicker.
- **Sufficient Movement:** The chance of getting sick increases slightly, and recovery is slower. The impact on work is greater, and you experience more symptoms.
- **Insufficient Movement:** The chance of catching a cold is almost doubled. Half of the colds could be prevented this way.
- **Long Periods of Sitting:** The chance of getting sick is tripled. The risk of more serious infections, such as pneumonia, increases.

**Read More:** In one study, colds occurred 3 times more often in the group with no movement. Movement also lowers inflammatory substances, and chronic inflammation contributes to diseases like arthritis.

## Day 6: Movement and Mental Health

> **Saying:** 'Walking in circles.'

**Summary:** Highlights the crucial role of movement in influencing mental well-being, coping with stress, and being effective in the treatment of mental health conditions.

### Sections
- **Stress:** Movement releases hormones that reduce anxiety, stress, and uncertainty. This makes us more resilient to stressful situations.
- **Fatigue:** The fatigue from exercise is short-lived and provides more energy for the rest of the day. People who move more have more energy and can maintain their attention longer.
- **Difficulty with Life:** As fatigue increases, it becomes harder to keep daily matters in order, leading to concentration problems and a rut.
- **Self-Worth:** Struggling to keep up has a negative effect on self-worth and well-being, leading to feelings of worthlessness.
- **Anxiety Disorder:** Movement is protective and helps with anxiety disorders, giving more control and reducing the burden of illness.
- **Depression:** Sufficient movement lowers the risk of depression. Running is even a form of therapy because it releases hormones that increase feelings of happiness.

**Read More:** Movement is an essential part of the treatment for burnout, depression, anxiety disorder, and ADHD. Prevention is better than cure.

## Day 7: Types of Training

> **Saying:** 'Making strides.'

**Summary:** Describes the three main forms of training â€“ Flexibility, Cardio, and Strength â€“ and emphasizes that a combination of these is most effective for long-term improvement.

### Sections
- **Flexibility:** Exercises like Yoga, Pilates, dancing, or gymnastics improve your posture, balance, and stability. Should be done at least twice a week, or every morning.
- **Cardio Training:** Endurance training that increases heart rate, strengthening your fitness. Examples include running and cycling. At least 5 days a week for 30 minutes of light exercise and 2 days for 1 hour of intensive exercise.
- **Strength Training:** Strengthens muscles and increases fat burning. Larger muscle mass burns more calories daily and makes daily tasks easier. Should not be missing from a weight loss exercise plan.
- **Combination:** Combining the different types of training works best. Make a mix of activities you enjoy, and working together often helps.
- **Building Up:** Keep it realistic. Fitness, strength, and flexibility must be built up step by step. It's about the long term, not the short term.

**Read More:** Adults should get 150 minutes of moderate or vigorous-intensity exercise spread over several days, plus bone and muscle-strengthening activities twice a week and balance exercises twice a week. Avoid prolonged sitting.

## Day 8: Disadvantages of Sitting

> **Saying:** 'Standstill.'

**Summary:** Warns about the adverse effects of prolonged sitting on the entire body, from the neck and back to the intestines and ankles, and provides concrete tips to break this habit.

### Sections
- **Neck Complaints and Headaches:** Often the result of hours in a tense posture at a desk. Changes to the skull can even be seen from staring at mobile phones.
- **Back Complaints:** Prolonged sitting keeps back muscles in the same position, causing continuous strain and affecting blood flow. The risk increases when alternated with heavy lifting without a warm-up.
- **Intestinal Complaints:** Longer sitting increases the risk of colon cancer and constipation, as movement stimulates the intestines and propels stool.
- **Joint Complaints:** Joints become stiff and joint fluid decreases, leading to stiff, painful joints and an increased risk of wear and tear.
- **Swollen Ankles and Varicose Veins:** Walking pumps blood back to the heart. Prolonged sitting leads to fluid retention in the legs, damaged veins, and an increased risk of varicose veins.

**Read More:** Tips: Set up your workspace ergonomically. Check your posture every half hour. Do 2 minutes of small stretching exercises every half hour. Walk a lap or take the stairs between tasks. Drink enough water and get it frequently. Have walking meetings instead of sitting meetings.

## Day 10: Limitations in Movement

> **Saying:** 'Taking a step back.'

**Summary:** Advises on dealing with movement limitations due to injuries, chronic conditions like arthritis and rheumatism, and disabilities, with an emphasis on professional guidance and continuing to train what is possible.

### Sections
- **Injuries:** Very restrictive; it helps to be guided by a professional and to continue training your uninjured muscles and joints during recovery.
- **Arthritis:** Gradual movement is often preferred to prevent surgery. Aids can help you stay active longer.
- **Rheumatism:** Seeking help is important for inflamed, painful joints. Movement is an important part of the treatment for various forms of rheumatism.
- **Disability:** There are increasingly more opportunities for people with disabilities to play sports. Don't let the barrier to using aids hold you back.

**Read More:** With the loss of mobility, good recovery and keeping what you can fit is essential. Building up after recovery prevents relapse and is a wise investment. A fixed exercise program helps to manage many musculoskeletal conditions.

## Day 11: Movement and Your Brain

> **Saying:** 'A healthy mind in a healthy body.'

**Summary:** Confirms the strong connection between movement and the brain. Physical exercise improves mental performance, memory, and mood, and protects against brain aging and neurological disorders.

### Sections
- **Learning and Mental Performance:** Physical exercise improves learning and mental performance; fitness is linked to a higher IQ, better school results, and reaction time. This improvement is found in everyone who starts moving more.
- **Memory:** Movement increases activity in the temporal lobe, where memory is stored. More connections are made, making information easier to retrieve.
- **Hormones:** Endorphins and GABA are produced, which reduce pain, create a feeling of happiness, and help reduce stress, anxiety, and depression.
- **Disease and Aging:** Reduces the chance of brain aging, dementia, and Alzheimer's disease. Also slows the decline of coordination in Parkinson's disease.
- **Attention and Concentration:** Increases in young people after just 10 minutes of sports and reduces symptoms in children with ADHD.
- **Brain Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF):** More of this substance in people who move more, which stimulates the nervous system to repair and even grow.
- **Blood Vessels:** The blood vessels in the brain also benefit, ensuring a better supply of nutrients and oxygen, and thus better function.

**Read More:** For studying or better performance at work, you should plan both studying and physical exercise. Every movement helps; you don't have to become a top athlete right away.

## Day 12: Energy and Movement

> **Saying:** 'Step by step.'

**Summary:** Provides an indication of the calorie consumption of various everyday and sporting activities, noting that the actual consumption depends on weight, muscle mass, and intensity.

### Sections
- **Walking:** About 400 calories in 80 minutes (about a slice of chocolate cake).
- **Cycling:** About 530 calories in 60 minutes of gentle cycling (about 3 fried eggs with bacon and cheese).
- **Running:** About 190 calories in 20 minutes (about 1 can of coke).
- **Fitness:** About 145 calories in 20 minutes (about 1 bag of chips).
- **Housework:** About 450 calories in 130 minutes (about 1 hamburger bun).
- **Swimming:** About 320 calories in 40 minutes (about 2 cheese sandwiches).

**Read More:** There is a big difference between burning calories with movement and with thinking. Physical exercise stimulates all blood vessels and grows muscles; both (thinking and moving) are recommended.

## Day 13: The Right Fuel

> **Saying:** 'The right fuel for movement and nutrition.'

**Summary:** Focuses on the optimal nutrition strategy around exercise, with advice on timing and the type of food (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) for maximum energy and recovery.

### Sections
- **Eating Before Exercise:** The best time is between 1 to 3 hours before exercise. Avoid fatty foods and too much protein, as they take longer to digest and hinder performance. Carbohydrates are good as they release energy gradually.
- **During Exercise:** You want to get sugars in quickly, for example, with fruit (banana) or dextrose. Ensure a gradual and regular supply of sugar, for example, through carbohydrates, because pure sugar is quickly used up after a peak.
- **After Exercise (Recovery):** Good to replenish reserves and consumed substances: Vitamin C for damage, proteins to repair muscle cells, and fats and sugars for energy.
- **Hydration:** Don't forget to drink enough water before and after exercise, as your body will recover less well otherwise.

**Read More:** If you have just eaten, more blood goes to your intestines and your nervous system adjusts to rest and digestion, which inhibits performance. During recovery, muscle and connective tissue are repaired stronger; proteins, vitamins, and antioxidants are important then.

## Day 14: Maintenance

> **Saying:** 'Holding your ground.'

**Summary:** Provides strategies to avoid falling back after building up fitness. This requires planning, using resources, and setting challenges to stay motivated.

### Sections
- **Regular Schedule:** To avoid falling back, schedule your movement at a lower intensity instead of relying on spontaneous moments.
- **Weather:** Don't let the weather determine your fitness; think of weather-independent alternatives.
- **Vacations:** Ideal moments to work on your fitness. Plan physical activities like brisk walks or swimming.
- **Challenges:** Participating in a strenuous activity twice a year helps to stay in shape and motivates you to persevere.
- **Help:** You don't have to do it alone; working with family, a club, a coach, or friends works.
- **Other Motivation:** Put something at stake: promise to do something unpleasant if you don't stick to a plan. Donating money to a bad cause is a stronger motivator than paying, as you are not just buying it off.

**Read More:** To change behavior, you need to plan things and take them as seriously as your work or a doctor's prescription.
